What does it mean when you hear a company is worth $X billion?

There are several ways of valuing a company:

    Comparable companies
    Precedent transactions
    Leveraged buyout
    Discounted cash flow

 

A DCF tries to figure out how much a company is worth today based on how much money it is projected to make in the future.

Described as “discounted” cash flow because cash now is worth more than cash in the future.

DCFs use this principle by summing the discounted, predicted free cash flows of a company to present time

 

Think of it this way:

Net Present Value (NPV) = Cash Flow1 / (1+r) + Cash Flow2 / (1+r)2 ... + Cash Flown / (1+r)n

 

Valuation: Key Terms

Equity Value: how much a company is worth (sticker price)

    Equity Value = Outstanding Shares * Share Price

Enterprise Value: how much a company costs to acquire

    EV = Equity Value - Cash + Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interests

EBIT: Earnings Before Interest and Tax (Operating Income)

    EBIT = Revenue - COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) - Operating Expenses

EBITDA: Earnings Before Interest Tax Depreciation & Amortization

    EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation & Amortization

FCF: Free Cash Flow

    FCF = EBIT (1 - tax rate) + Depreciation & Amortization - Change in NWC (Net Working Capital) - CapEx (Capital Expenditure)

 

The Importance of the Income Statement in the DCF

Income Statement

Revenue - COGS = Gross Revenue

Gross Revenue - SG&A (Selling, General and Administrative Expenses) = EBITDA

EBITDA - Depreciation & Amortization = EBIT

EBIT - Interest Expense = EBT

EBT - Tax Expense = Net Income

 

Overview of Steps in Calculating a DCF:

1. Establish timeframe

2. Forecast revenue growth and operating expenses

3. Calculate free cash flows

4. Calculate discount rate

5. Find present value of future cash flows

6. Calculate terminal value

7. Calculate enterprise value

8. Find implied share price
